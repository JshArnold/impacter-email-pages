What If We Stopped Asking Students To Rate Their Feelings From One To Five?

A different approach to behavioral health screening — one that actually listens.

The Problem

For more than a decade, districts across America have used Likert-scale surveys to understand student wellbeing. A 1 asks students to locate a feeling on a number line. A 5 suggests they have it figured out. But a child in crisis rarely answers 5. And a child who is quietly struggling never fills it in at all. We have been asking the wrong question.

The instruments we rely on were designed to aggregate, not to understand. They tell a district something happened without telling anyone what. A percentage can flag a trend. It cannot explain why Marcus stopped eating lunch with his friends, or why Aaliyah's grades collapsed in November, or why a student who wrote "I'm fine" on every screener for three years was anything but.

Something shifts when a student is given a microphone instead of a rubric. They stop performing wellness and start describing it. The hesitation before a sentence. The qualifier that reveals the real belief. The pivot from what they planned to say to what they actually mean. This is the signal behavioral health has always been missing — and the one that AI is now capable of hearing.

MTSS Integration

How districts build it into their existing architecture.

1

Universal — Tier 1

The Screener runs district-wide, three times a year. Every student responds. Every voice is heard. The model identifies students who warrant a closer look — without stigma, without opt-in, without a counselor having to guess.

2

Targeted — Tier 2

Students flagged at Tier 1 are routed to small-group support. The Screener data informs the grouping — counselors work with students who have similar behavioral health profiles, not arbitrary scheduling cohorts.

3

Intensive — Tier 3

For students in acute need, the Screener provides the clinical team with documented voice-based evidence — transcript, domain scores, and AI-generated summary — before the first appointment. Less intake. More intervention.

The Funding Mechanism

California is paying for districts to listen better. Here's how.

California's Children and Youth Behavioral Health Initiative — CYBHI — created a funding mechanism that most district business offices have not yet learned to use. The mechanism is straightforward: the Behavioral Health Screener qualifies for reimbursement under CPT code 96127 with diagnosis code Z13.89, payable by Medi-Cal, private insurance, and county mental health plans.

The reimbursement rate is $4.81 per administration. For a district of 5,000 students screening three times a year, that is $72,150 in recoverable revenue annually — enough to cover the Screener and fund a portion of the counseling hours the data generates. Three practitioner types can administer: licensed clinical social workers, credentialed school counselors, and licensed marriage and family therapists. Most districts already employ at least two of the three.

The practical result is a screening program that costs $0 net after reimbursement — and generates both student data and a funding stream that grows with usage. The districts that have moved fastest are not the ones with the largest behavioral health budgets. They are the ones whose business offices read the CPT schedule.

State Validation

Selected by California for statewide evaluation.

In 2024, the California Behavioral Health Services Oversight and Accountability Commission — BHSOAC — partnered with the Santa Clara County Office of Education to identify tools capable of anchoring statewide behavioral health performance management. The Behavioral Health Screener was selected under designation BHSSA-002.

Selection does not mean endorsement. It means the instrument met a threshold: the Commission believed the Screener was capable of producing behavioral health evidence at scale, reliably enough to inform policy. That is a different standard than a vendor claim or a pilot anecdote.

For districts considering the Screener, BHSSA-002 provides one practical assurance: this instrument has been examined by people whose job is to evaluate behavioral health instruments. It held up.
