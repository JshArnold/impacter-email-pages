What districts are discovering

Behavioral health data has always been in the room. Now we can hear it.

For the first time, the behavioral health of a student can be drawn from how they naturally express themselves — in voice, in writing, in their own language. Scored against research-based competencies. Structured enough to act on. Districts across California are already using this data, presenting it to their boards, and holding themselves accountable to what they find.

For decades, the primary instrument for measuring student wellness was a survey with a five-point scale — anonymous, aggregated, useful at the district level but silent about the student in front of you. What follows are four public board meetings where California districts grappled openly with what comes next.

Central Union High School District Imperial Valley Board Presentation

Before the board presentation, a teacher flagged something to the mental health specialist. A student had revealed, through their IMPACTER response, something they hadn't said to anyone in person. The family was contacted. What the board heard next was that this wasn't a one-off — it was what becomes possible when students have a way to express themselves outside of a face-to-face interaction, at any hour.

San Diego Unified Board Meeting April 29, 2026

San Diego Unified's board convened for a formal progress monitoring report on student motivation — a governance guardrail the board had adopted three years earlier. The cabinet presented voice intelligence data from 31,469 students across 10 schools. They played a student's audio response live. Then they made a proposal: that the motivation guardrail be folded into the district's wellness goal — because the data had revealed that the two were the same thing.

Board President Barrera: "We need better tools than the California Healthy Kids Survey. This tool that you're demonstrating — everybody up here has said it's a breakthrough."

What this makes possible

A new layer of student data — in the hands of teachers, principals, and boards.

  • 01
    Board-level accountability with real data Boards adopting student outcomes focused governance frameworks finally have behavioral health metrics worth monitoring — drawn from student voice, scored against competency rubrics, structured for governance reporting.
  • 02
    Behavioral health data that informs academic intervention A student struggling with reading may have a gap in persistence — not phonics. A student disengaged from math may have low curiosity scores. This data now reaches teachers and principals, not just counselors.
  • 03
    A replacement for aggregated, anonymous surveys California's leading districts are moving beyond instruments that tell you how a district feels to instruments that tell you how a student feels — actionable, individual, and gathered in the student's own words.
  • 04
    A foundation for behavioral health screening California mandated dyslexia screening. A math screener is coming. The same logic — early identification, actionable data, tied to academic outcomes — applies to behavioral health. The infrastructure now exists to do it.
The tool behind what you just watched

IMPACTER Pathway

IMPACTER is a voice intelligence platform built for K–12. Students respond to structured prompts in their own words — spoken, written, or in any of several languages. Every response is scored against eight research-based competencies: purpose, grit, growth mindset, curiosity, self-control, gratitude, compassion, and perspective-taking.

The resulting data lives in district and teacher dashboards — structured, searchable, and designed to inform both wellness goals and academic intervention. It is currently deployed in school districts across California, including the ones featured in this collection.
