The shift

A Likert scale tells you what students are willing to say. Voice tells you what they actually mean.

For decades, the primary instrument for understanding student wellbeing was a rating scale — anonymous, aggregated, useful at the district level but silent about the student in front of you.

Something changed. Voice intelligence can now score student speech against research-based behavioral health competencies — purpose, grit, curiosity, self-control, belonging. Structured enough to act on. Individual enough to matter.

The following four board meetings document what happens when districts start using it.

San Diego Unified School District Governance Workshop · 2025
"I left really jazzed. I came straight into your office and said: I can't wait till you see this."
Superintendent Lamont Jackson · San Diego Unified

San Diego Unified was building a district wellness goal — and the question before the board was whether any tool could actually measure what they cared about. Not test scores. Not attendance. How students see themselves, and whether they feel they belong.

Superintendent Jackson came out of a meeting with IMPACTER and went straight to a colleague's office. She described what she saw: a measurement tool built for how students actually think — with the ease of something they'd already use. Modern. Quick. Built around curiosity and purpose, not checkboxes.

Her team's language shifted from "measuring wellness" to "transforming how we think about counseling — in service of social emotional wellness, but also academics."

Before the board presentation, a teacher noticed something in a student's IMPACTER response — something the student hadn't said to anyone face to face. The teacher flagged it to the mental health specialist. The family was contacted. The student got help.

It was only possible because the platform is available at any hour, in any language, without the pressure of a face-to-face interaction. The student found a way to say what they couldn't say in person.

The board heard the story. Then they heard the full presentation: Harvard's eight behavioral health competencies — compassion, curiosity, gratitude, grit, growth mindset, perspective-taking, purpose, self-control — scored from natural language, in real time, across an entire district.

San Mateo-Foster City School District Board Meeting
"Your voice is making me cry."
Trustee · San Mateo-Foster City School District

The board asked to hear actual student voice. They played two student responses — one in English, one in Spanish. The second was from an elementary student who had switched schools to escape bullying. He described what belonging felt like when he finally found it, and named the teacher who had changed his path.

The room went quiet. Then a trustee spoke.

The superintendent described what was happening in systems terms: "We're going beyond what 99.9% of other districts do. We send out generic surveys and force people to give us an answer we want from them. This opens up the floodgates to hear what they really think — and uses machine learning to find the themes that actually dictate what we do next."

San Diego Unified School District Board Meeting · April 29, 2026
"We can't answer 'why' with a five-point Likert. We can begin to answer 'why' with student voice."
Dr. Roman Del Rosario · Cabinet Presenter · San Diego Unified
Public board meeting · April 29, 2026 · Watch on YouTube ↗

The board convened for a formal progress monitoring report on student motivation — a governance guardrail adopted three years earlier. The cabinet presented voice intelligence data from 31,469 students across 10 pilot schools. They played a student's audio response live: Christopher, an 11th grader, describing what actually motivates him to come to school — including fear and love, things no survey was designed to capture.

Board President Barrera: "We need better tools than the California Healthy Kids Survey. This tool — everybody up here has said it's a breakthrough."

The board then discussed folding the student motivation guardrail into the district's Wellness Goal — because the data had revealed they were measuring the same thing from two angles. The vote is expected at the SOFG workshop this month.

What this makes possible

New data. In the hands of the people who can use it.

01
Board governance with real accountability
Districts adopting student outcomes focused governance finally have behavioral health metrics worth monitoring. Drawn from student voice, scored against competency rubrics, structured for a board monitoring report.
02
Behavioral health data that reaches teachers
A struggling reader may have a gap in persistence — not phonics. A student disengaged from math may have low curiosity scores. This data now reaches teachers and principals, not just counselors. The answer to an academic problem isn't always an academic answer.
03
A foundation for behavioral health screening
California mandated dyslexia screening. A math screener is coming. The same logic — early identification, actionable data, tied to academic outcomes — applies to behavioral health. The infrastructure now exists to do it at scale.
04
A replacement for aggregated, anonymous data
California's leading districts are moving beyond instruments that tell you how a district feels toward instruments that tell you how a student feels — individual, actionable, and gathered in the student's own words.
The tool behind what you just watched

IMPACTER Pathway

IMPACTER is a voice intelligence platform built for K–12. Students respond to structured prompts in their own words — spoken, written, or in any of several languages. Every response is scored against eight research-based competencies grounded in Harvard research and aligned to the CASEL framework.

The resulting data lives in district and teacher dashboards — structured, searchable, and designed to inform both wellness goals and academic intervention. Currently deployed across California, including the districts above.
